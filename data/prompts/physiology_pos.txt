Cardiac output equals stroke volume multiplied by heart rate and is approximately 5 liters per minute at rest.
The Frank-Starling mechanism states that stroke volume increases with increased ventricular preload due to greater sarcomere stretch.
Mean arterial pressure equals cardiac output multiplied by total peripheral resistance.
The sinoatrial node is the primary pacemaker of the heart with an intrinsic rate of 60 to 100 beats per minute.
The action potential of ventricular cardiomyocytes has a prolonged plateau phase due to L-type calcium channel opening.
Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch detect changes in arterial pressure and modulate autonomic output.
Sympathetic stimulation increases heart rate and contractility via beta-1 adrenergic receptors and cAMP-mediated phosphorylation.
Afterload is the resistance against which the ventricle must eject blood and is primarily determined by systemic vascular resistance.
The ejection fraction is the proportion of end-diastolic volume ejected per beat and is normally greater than 55 percent.
Starling forces govern fluid movement across capillary walls, with hydrostatic pressure favoring filtration and oncotic pressure favoring reabsorption.
Tidal volume is the volume of air inhaled or exhaled in a single normal breath, approximately 500 mL at rest.
The functional residual capacity is the volume remaining in the lungs after a normal expiration and equals ERV plus RV.
Alveolar ventilation equals tidal volume minus dead space volume multiplied by respiratory rate.
Oxygen is transported primarily bound to hemoglobin, with only about 1.5 percent dissolved in plasma.
The oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve shifts right with increased temperature, CO2, 2,3-DPG, and decreased pH.
Carbon dioxide is transported mainly as bicarbonate in plasma, formed by carbonic anhydrase in red blood cells.
The Hering-Breuer reflex limits excessive lung inflation via pulmonary stretch receptors that inhibit further inspiration.
Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction diverts blood away from poorly ventilated lung regions to optimize ventilation-perfusion matching.
The ventilation-perfusion ratio is highest at the lung apex and lowest at the base due to gravitational effects on blood flow.
Compliance is the change in lung volume per unit change in pressure and is reduced in fibrosis and increased in emphysema.
The glomerular filtration rate is approximately 125 mL per minute and represents the volume of plasma filtered per unit time.
Sodium reabsorption in the proximal convoluted tubule is coupled to glucose, amino acids, and phosphate via cotransporters.
Aldosterone acts on principal cells of the collecting duct to increase sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion.
ADH increases water permeability of the collecting duct by inserting aquaporin-2 channels into the apical membrane.
The countercurrent multiplier in the loop of Henle generates the medullary osmotic gradient necessary for urine concentration.
Autoregulation maintains relatively constant GFR over a wide range of arterial pressures via myogenic and tubuloglomerular feedback.
Renin is secreted by juxtaglomerular cells in response to decreased renal perfusion pressure and sympathetic stimulation.
The proximal tubule reabsorbs approximately 67 percent of filtered sodium, water, and bicarbonate isosmotically.
Titratable acids and ammonium are the two primary mechanisms by which the kidney excretes protons in the urine.
Creatinine clearance is used as a clinical approximation of GFR because creatinine is freely filtered and minimally secreted.
The resting membrane potential of neurons is approximately -70 mV, maintained by the sodium-potassium ATPase and potassium leak channels.
An action potential is initiated when depolarization reaches threshold, triggering rapid opening of voltage-gated sodium channels.
The absolute refractory period corresponds to sodium channel inactivation and prevents re-excitation regardless of stimulus strength.
Myelination increases conduction velocity by allowing saltatory conduction between nodes of Ranvier.
Synaptic transmission involves calcium-dependent fusion of synaptic vesicles with the presynaptic membrane and neurotransmitter release.
Long-term potentiation requires NMDA receptor activation and is widely regarded as a cellular basis of learning and memory.
The cerebellum coordinates fine motor movements and compares intended with actual movement to correct motor errors.
The basal ganglia modulate voluntary movement through direct and indirect pathways, both involving dopamine from the substantia nigra.
The dorsal column-medial lemniscal pathway carries fine touch, proprioception, and vibration sensation from the ipsilateral body.
The spinothalamic tract carries pain and temperature sensation from the contralateral body after decussating in the spinal cord.
Gastric acid secretion by parietal cells is stimulated by acetylcholine, histamine, and gastrin acting on their respective receptors.
Intrinsic factor is secreted by gastric parietal cells and is essential for vitamin B12 absorption in the terminal ileum.
Cholecystokinin is released from I cells in the duodenum in response to fat and protein, stimulating pancreatic enzyme secretion.
Secretin is released from S cells in the duodenum in response to luminal acid, stimulating pancreatic bicarbonate secretion.
The migrating motor complex clears the gut between meals and is mediated by motilin released from the small intestine.
Bile salts are synthesized from cholesterol in the liver, conjugated with glycine or taurine, and secreted into bile.
Fat absorption requires emulsification by bile salts, digestion by pancreatic lipase, and packaging into chylomicrons.
Glucose and galactose are absorbed by SGLT1 cotransporters in the intestinal brush border membrane.
The enteric nervous system contains as many neurons as the spinal cord and can function independently of central nervous input.
Gastrin is secreted by G cells in the antrum of the stomach and stimulates acid secretion and gastric motility.
Insulin is secreted by pancreatic beta cells in response to elevated blood glucose and promotes glycogen synthesis and fat storage.
Glucagon is secreted by alpha cells during fasting and stimulates hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.
Cortisol is a glucocorticoid secreted by the zona fasciculata that promotes gluconeogenesis and suppresses inflammation.
Growth hormone stimulates IGF-1 production in the liver and promotes lipolysis and protein anabolism.
Thyroid hormones increase basal metabolic rate, cardiac output, and oxygen consumption in virtually all tissues.
Parathyroid hormone increases serum calcium by stimulating bone resorption, renal calcium reabsorption, and calcitriol synthesis.
Calcitonin is secreted by parafollicular C cells in response to hypercalcemia and inhibits osteoclast-mediated bone resorption.
ADH is synthesized in the hypothalamus, stored in the posterior pituitary, and released in response to increased plasma osmolality.
The hypothalamic-pituitary axis regulates most endocrine glands through releasing and inhibiting hormones via the portal circulation.
Estrogen promotes the LH surge at midcycle by positive feedback on the hypothalamus and anterior pituitary during the follicular phase.
Skeletal muscle contraction is triggered by calcium binding to troponin C, displacing tropomyosin and exposing actin binding sites.
The neuromuscular junction uses acetylcholine as its neurotransmitter, which binds nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate.
Excitation-contraction coupling links membrane depolarization to calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum via T-tubules and ryanodine receptors.
The sliding filament theory describes muscle contraction as actin and myosin filaments sliding past each other without shortening.
Smooth muscle contraction is regulated by calcium-calmodulin activation of myosin light chain kinase.
Type I slow-twitch muscle fibers are fatigue-resistant, rely on oxidative metabolism, and contain high levels of myoglobin.
Type II fast-twitch fibers contract rapidly, fatigue quickly, and rely primarily on glycolytic metabolism for energy production.
Rigor mortis results from depletion of ATP after death, causing permanent cross-bridge formation between actin and myosin.
The length-tension relationship shows that maximum force is generated at optimal sarcomere length where actin-myosin overlap is greatest.
Motor unit recruitment follows the size principle, with small slow-twitch units recruited first and large fast-twitch units recruited last.
Erythropoietin is produced by peritubular cells of the kidney in response to hypoxia and stimulates red cell production in bone marrow.
Iron is absorbed in the ferrous form in the duodenum and is transported in plasma bound to transferrin.
The intrinsic pathway of coagulation is initiated by contact activation of factor XII and leads to factor X activation.
Platelets are activated by collagen, thrombin, and ADP, leading to shape change, degranulation, and aggregation.
Neutrophils are the most abundant white blood cells and are the first responders to bacterial infection.
Hemoglobin F has higher oxygen affinity than hemoglobin A due to gamma chains that bind 2,3-DPG less avidly than beta chains.
Von Willebrand factor bridges platelet glycoprotein Ib and subendothelial collagen during primary hemostasis.
The extrinsic pathway is initiated by tissue factor exposure and is the primary physiological pathway of coagulation in vivo.
Thrombopoietin is produced by the liver and kidneys and regulates platelet production from megakaryocytes in the bone marrow.
The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation relates blood pH to the ratio of bicarbonate to dissolved carbon dioxide.
Respiratory acidosis is characterized by elevated PCO2 and decreased pH, compensated by renal bicarbonate retention.
Metabolic alkalosis results from excess bicarbonate or loss of acid and is compensated by hypoventilation to retain CO2.
The anion gap equals sodium minus the sum of chloride and bicarbonate and is elevated in certain metabolic acidoses.
Hypokalemia causes hyperpolarization of cell membranes and can lead to muscle weakness and cardiac arrhythmias.
Hypernatremia indicates a deficit of free water relative to sodium and must be corrected slowly to prevent cerebral edema.
The kidneys regulate acid-base balance by varying bicarbonate reabsorption and proton secretion along the tubules.
Calcium exists in three plasma forms: protein-bound, complexed, and ionized, with only the ionized fraction being physiologically active.
Aldosterone promotes sodium retention and potassium excretion; hyperaldosteronism causes hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis.
The menstrual cycle averages 28 days and is regulated by cyclic changes in FSH, LH, estrogen, and progesterone.
Ovulation is triggered by the LH surge occurring approximately 24 to 36 hours before follicle rupture at midcycle.
Progesterone is secreted by the corpus luteum after ovulation and prepares the endometrium for potential implantation.
Human chorionic gonadotropin is secreted by the trophoblast after implantation and maintains the corpus luteum in early pregnancy.
Testosterone is synthesized from cholesterol in Leydig cells under stimulation by luteinizing hormone.
Spermatogenesis occurs in the seminiferous tubules and is supported by Sertoli cells, which secrete inhibin and androgen-binding protein.
The blood-testis barrier is formed by tight junctions between Sertoli cells and protects developing spermatocytes from immune attack.
Estrogen at high sustained levels exerts positive feedback on the pituitary to trigger the preovulatory LH surge.
FSH stimulates follicular development and granulosa cell aromatase activity, converting androgens to estrogens.
Oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions during labor and milk ejection during breastfeeding via a positive feedback mechanism.
