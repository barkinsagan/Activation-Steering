Glycolysis converts one molecule of glucose to two molecules of pyruvate, producing a net yield of 2 ATP and 2 NADH.
The rate-limiting step of glycolysis is catalyzed by phosphofructokinase-1, which is inhibited by ATP and citrate.
The tricarboxylic acid cycle produces 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, and 1 GTP per turn of the cycle.
The rate-limiting step of the TCA cycle is the isocitrate dehydrogenase reaction, producing NADH and CO2.
Oxidative phosphorylation occurs in the inner mitochondrial membrane and generates approximately 32 ATP per glucose molecule.
The pentose phosphate pathway produces NADPH and ribose-5-phosphate, branching off from glycolysis at glucose-6-phosphate.
Gluconeogenesis is the synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors and primarily occurs in the liver.
Glycogen synthase is the key enzyme in glycogen synthesis and requires UDP-glucose as the activated glucose donor.
The Cori cycle transfers lactate from muscles to the liver, where it is converted back to glucose via gluconeogenesis.
Pyruvate dehydrogenase converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, linking glycolysis to the TCA cycle and requiring thiamine pyrophosphate.
Transamination transfers an amino group from an amino acid to alpha-ketoglutarate, forming glutamate and requiring pyridoxal phosphate.
The urea cycle converts toxic ammonia to urea for excretion and occurs partly in the mitochondria and partly in the cytoplasm.
Phenylketonuria results from deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase, causing accumulation of phenylalanine and intellectual disability.
Homocystinuria is caused by cystathionine beta-synthase deficiency, leading to elevated homocysteine in blood and urine.
Maple syrup urine disease results from branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase deficiency, affecting leucine, isoleucine, and valine catabolism.
Tyrosine is a conditionally essential amino acid derived from phenylalanine and is a precursor for catecholamines and melanin.
Glutamate dehydrogenase catalyzes the oxidative deamination of glutamate, releasing free ammonia and producing alpha-ketoglutarate.
Alkaptonuria is caused by homogentisate oxidase deficiency, leading to accumulation of homogentisic acid and dark urine.
The transsulfuration pathway converts homocysteine to cysteine via cystathionine and requires vitamin B6 as a cofactor.
Tryptophan is a precursor for serotonin, melatonin, and niacin and is an essential amino acid that cannot be synthesized in humans.
Fatty acid synthesis occurs in the cytoplasm and requires NADPH and malonyl-CoA as the two-carbon donor.
Acetyl-CoA carboxylase catalyzes the rate-limiting step of fatty acid synthesis, converting acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA.
Beta-oxidation of fatty acids occurs in the mitochondrial matrix and produces acetyl-CoA, NADH, and FADH2 per cycle.
Carnitine shuttles long-chain fatty acids across the inner mitochondrial membrane for beta-oxidation.
Ketogenesis occurs in the liver during fasting when acetyl-CoA accumulates and oxaloacetate is diverted to gluconeogenesis.
HMG-CoA reductase is the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis and is inhibited by statins.
Lipoprotein lipase hydrolyzes triglycerides in chylomicrons and VLDL in peripheral tissues, releasing fatty acids for uptake.
LDL delivers cholesterol to peripheral tissues by binding the LDL receptor, which is internalized by receptor-mediated endocytosis.
HDL performs reverse cholesterol transport, collecting cholesterol from peripheral tissues and returning it to the liver.
Familial hypercholesterolemia is caused by mutations in the LDL receptor gene, leading to markedly elevated plasma LDL cholesterol.
Purines are synthesized de novo using a ribose-5-phosphate backbone with atoms contributed by glycine, glutamine, and other donors.
The rate-limiting step of purine synthesis is catalyzed by PRPP amidotransferase, which is inhibited by AMP and GMP.
Pyrimidines are synthesized before attachment to ribose, unlike purines which are built directly on the ribose scaffold.
HGPRT salvages hypoxanthine and guanine back to IMP and GMP respectively, using PRPP as the ribose donor.
Lesch-Nyhan syndrome results from HGPRT deficiency, causing hyperuricemia, gout, self-mutilation, and intellectual disability.
Xanthine oxidase catalyzes the conversion of hypoxanthine to xanthine and xanthine to uric acid in purine catabolism.
Orotic aciduria is caused by UMP synthase deficiency, leading to orotic acid accumulation and megaloblastic anemia.
Thymidylate synthase converts dUMP to dTMP using methylenetetrahydrofolate as the methyl donor.
Ribonucleotide reductase converts ribonucleoside diphosphates to deoxyribonucleoside diphosphates for DNA synthesis.
Hydroxyurea inhibits ribonucleotide reductase and is used to reduce sickling frequency in sickle cell disease.
The primary structure of a protein is the linear sequence of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.
Alpha-helices are stabilized by hydrogen bonds between the carbonyl oxygen of one residue and the amide nitrogen four residues ahead.
Beta-pleated sheets are formed by hydrogen bonds between adjacent polypeptide strands running parallel or antiparallel.
Tertiary protein structure is stabilized by hydrophobic interactions, disulfide bonds, hydrogen bonds, and ionic interactions.
Chaperone proteins such as Hsp70 assist in the proper folding of newly synthesized polypeptides and prevent aggregation.
Prions are misfolded proteins that induce the misfolding of normal PrPc into the pathological PrPsc conformation.
Amyloid fibrils form from beta-sheet rich aggregates and are associated with Alzheimer's disease and type 2 diabetes.
Collagen is a triple-helical structural protein that requires hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues, which depends on vitamin C.
Hemoglobin exhibits cooperative oxygen binding due to conformational changes that increase affinity as successive subunits bind oxygen.
The Bohr effect describes the decreased oxygen affinity of hemoglobin in the presence of increased CO2 and decreased pH.
Michaelis-Menten kinetics describes enzyme activity in terms of Km, the substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity.
Competitive inhibitors increase the apparent Km without affecting Vmax, and their effect can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration.
Non-competitive inhibitors decrease Vmax without affecting Km, as they bind a site distinct from the active site.
Allosteric enzymes exhibit sigmoidal kinetics and are regulated by molecules binding at sites other than the active site.
Covalent modification such as phosphorylation by kinases is a rapid and reversible mechanism for regulating enzyme activity.
Zymogen activation involves proteolytic cleavage of an inactive precursor, as seen with pepsinogen converting to pepsin.
Feedback inhibition occurs when the end product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an early enzyme in that pathway.
The turnover number (kcat) describes the maximum number of substrate molecules converted per enzyme molecule per second.
Isoenzymes are different molecular forms of an enzyme that catalyze the same reaction but differ in kinetics and tissue distribution.
Lactate dehydrogenase isoenzymes differ between heart (LDH1) and liver (LDH5) and are used in clinical diagnosis.
Thiamine (B1) is required as thiamine pyrophosphate by pyruvate dehydrogenase, alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, and transketolase.
Riboflavin (B2) is a precursor for FAD and FMN, which are cofactors in oxidation-reduction reactions in the electron transport chain.
Niacin (B3) is a precursor for NAD+ and NADP+, essential coenzymes in glycolysis, the TCA cycle, and fatty acid synthesis.
Pantothenate (B5) is a component of coenzyme A, required for acetyl-CoA formation, fatty acid synthesis, and the TCA cycle.
Pyridoxine (B6) as pyridoxal phosphate is required for transamination, decarboxylation, and glycogen phosphorylase reactions.
Biotin is a cofactor for carboxylase enzymes including acetyl-CoA carboxylase and pyruvate carboxylase.
Folate is required for one-carbon transfers and is essential for purine synthesis and thymidylate production.
Cobalamin (B12) is required for methionine synthase and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase and is absorbed bound to intrinsic factor.
Vitamin C is required for hydroxylation of proline and lysine in collagen synthesis and acts as a reducing antioxidant.
Vitamin K is a cofactor for gamma-carboxylation of glutamate residues in clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X.
DNA replication is semiconservative, meaning each daughter strand contains one original and one newly synthesized strand.
DNA polymerase III is the main replicative polymerase in prokaryotes and requires a primer to initiate strand synthesis.
Eukaryotic DNA replication initiates at multiple origins simultaneously and proceeds bidirectionally from each origin.
Transcription in eukaryotes is carried out by RNA pol I for rRNA, pol II for mRNA, and pol III for tRNA and snRNA.
The spliceosome removes introns from pre-mRNA and joins exons to produce mature mRNA in eukaryotic cells.
Translation initiation in eukaryotes requires the 5-prime cap, poly-A tail, and multiple eukaryotic initiation factors.
The genetic code is degenerate, meaning multiple codons can encode the same amino acid, but it is not ambiguous.
DNA repair mechanisms include nucleotide excision repair for bulky lesions and mismatch repair for replication errors.
Telomerase adds repetitive TTAGGG sequences to chromosome ends and is active in germ cells and most cancer cells.
G protein-coupled receptors activate adenylyl cyclase via Gs proteins, increasing cAMP and activating protein kinase A.
Receptor tyrosine kinases autophosphorylate upon ligand binding, activating downstream RAS-MAPK and PI3K-AKT pathways.
The phosphoinositide pathway generates IP3 and DAG from PIP2, leading to calcium release and protein kinase C activation.
JAK-STAT signaling is used by cytokine receptors and leads to direct transcriptional activation by phosphorylated STAT proteins.
RAS is a monomeric G protein that cycles between GDP-bound inactive and GTP-bound active states.
Inositol trisphosphate (IP3) binds receptors on the endoplasmic reticulum, triggering calcium release into the cytoplasm.
The WNT signaling pathway stabilizes beta-catenin by inhibiting the destruction complex, allowing nuclear gene activation.
Second messengers including cAMP, cGMP, calcium, and DAG amplify extracellular signals within the cell interior.
Gaucher disease is caused by glucocerebrosidase deficiency, leading to glucocerebroside accumulation in macrophages.
Niemann-Pick disease results from sphingomyelinase deficiency, causing sphingomyelin accumulation in lysosomes.
Tay-Sachs disease is caused by hexosaminidase A deficiency, leading to GM2 ganglioside accumulation and neurodegeneration.
Fabry disease results from alpha-galactosidase A deficiency, causing globotriaosylceramide accumulation in vascular endothelium.
Pompe disease is caused by acid alpha-glucosidase deficiency, leading to glycogen accumulation in lysosomes and cardiomyopathy.
Von Gierke disease results from glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency, causing fasting hypoglycemia and hepatomegaly.
McArdle disease is caused by muscle glycogen phosphorylase deficiency, presenting with exercise intolerance and myoglobinuria.
Galactosemia results from galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase deficiency, causing jaundice, cataracts, and intellectual disability.
Cystic fibrosis is caused by mutations in the CFTR gene encoding a chloride channel, leading to thick mucus secretions.
Wilson's disease results from ATP7B mutations causing copper accumulation in the liver and brain, with Kayser-Fleischer rings in the cornea.
